Unit 1 · MYP 1

Material World

States of matter · Particle theory · Mixtures · Separation · Measurements

Definitions — States of Matter & Particle Theory▼

Core definitions

Kinetic particle theory
All matter is made of tiny particles that are always moving. The more energy they have, the faster they move. This theory explains all physical properties of solids, liquids, and gases.
Solid
Particles are closely packed in a regular lattice and can only vibrate about fixed positions. Strong forces hold them together. Fixed shape and volume. Cannot be compressed.
Liquid
Particles are close together but randomly arranged. They can flow and slide past each other. Fixed volume but takes the shape of its container. Barely compressible.
Gas
Particles are far apart, moving rapidly in all directions. Forces between particles are negligible. No fixed shape or volume. Easily compressed. Fills any container it is placed in.
Melting point
The temperature at which a solid changes to a liquid. Energy breaks bonds between particles without raising temperature — shown as a flat section (plateau) on a heating curve.
Boiling point
The temperature at which a liquid changes to a gas throughout the bulk liquid. Not just at the surface (that is evaporation). Another plateau on the heating curve.
Sublimation
When a solid changes directly into a gas without passing through the liquid state. Example: dry ice (solid CO₂), iodine crystals when heated.
Diffusion
The net movement of particles from a region of high concentration to low concentration due to random particle motion, until the particles are evenly spread (equilibrium).
Latent heat
The energy absorbed or released during a change of state at constant temperature. It breaks or forms intermolecular bonds — it does not raise the temperature. This is why heating curves have flat sections.

Scientific explanations — the important ones

Why does temperature stay constant during melting? The heat energy being added is used entirely to break the intermolecular bonds holding the solid lattice together. None of the energy goes into increasing the kinetic energy of particles, so temperature does not rise until all the solid has melted.
Why do gases diffuse faster than liquids? Gas particles have much greater kinetic energy and are far apart — nothing obstructs them. In liquids, particles are close together and must push past each other, slowing diffusion significantly.
How does temperature affect diffusion rate? Higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy → they move faster → they spread out more quickly → diffusion rate increases.
Why are pure substances used as standards? A pure substance has a precise, fixed melting point. An impure substance melts over a range of temperatures (impurity particles disrupt the regular lattice, requiring less energy to break it) and at a lower temperature than the pure substance. This is the basis of the melting point purity test.

Criteria connections

Crit B: Design an investigation into diffusion — identify IV (e.g. temperature), DV (e.g. distance diffused in a set time), CVs (same particles, same distance, same concentration).
Crit C: Read and annotate a heating/cooling curve — label melting point, boiling point, flat sections, and explain what is happening to particles in each section.
Crit D: Evaluate the melting point as a test of purity — discuss what results would indicate a pure vs impure sample and any limitations of the method.
Definitions — Mixtures, Alloys & Types▼

Core definitions

Pure substance
Contains only one type of particle (one element or one compound). Has a sharp, fixed melting and boiling point. Examples: pure water, pure gold, pure NaCl.
Mixture
Contains two or more substances not chemically combined. Each component keeps its own properties. The components can be separated by physical methods. The composition is variable.
Solution
A homogeneous (uniform throughout) mixture where a solute is dissolved in a solvent. Transparent. Particles are evenly distributed at the molecular level. Cannot be separated by filtration.
Suspension
A heterogeneous mixture where insoluble solid particles are dispersed in a liquid. Cloudy appearance. Particles settle on standing. Can be separated by filtration. Example: muddy water.
Emulsion
Two immiscible liquids mixed together — one liquid is dispersed in droplets throughout the other. Example: milk (fat droplets in water), mayonnaise. Separates unless an emulsifier is present.
Alloy
A mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (metals or non-metals). Not a compound — no chemical bonds are formed. Examples: steel (iron + carbon), brass (copper + zinc), bronze (copper + tin).
Solute
The substance that dissolves in a solvent. Usually the smaller quantity. Example: salt in saltwater.
Solvent
The liquid in which the solute dissolves. Usually the larger quantity. Example: water in saltwater.

Scientific explanation — why alloys are stronger than pure metals

Structure explanation: In a pure metal, all atoms are the same size and arranged in neat, regular layers. These layers can easily slide over each other when a force is applied — this is why pure metals are soft and malleable. When a different-sized atom (from the added element) is introduced into the lattice, it distorts the regular structure. The irregular sizes mean the layers can no longer slide past each other as easily. This makes the alloy harder and stronger than either of its component metals alone.

Definitions — All Separation Techniques▼
Filtration
Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid. The filter paper has tiny pores — liquid molecules (filtrate) pass through but large solid particles (residue) are trapped. Principle: particle size difference.
Simple distillation
Separates a solvent from a solution (e.g. water from saltwater). The solution is heated → solvent evaporates → vapour travels to the condenser → cools back to liquid (distillate). Solute remains behind. Principle: difference in boiling point.
Fractional distillation
Separates a mixture of liquids with different boiling points. A fractionating column allows repeated evaporation/condensation — the lowest boiling point liquid reaches the top first. Used for crude oil and ethanol/water separation.
Chromatography
Separates mixtures of dissolved substances (e.g. dyes, inks). Mobile phase (solvent) carries substances up the stationary phase (paper or silica). Substances that are more soluble or less attracted to stationary phase travel further. Principle: different solubility and attraction.
Rf value
A number between 0 and 1 used to identify a substance in chromatography. Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent front. The same substance always has the same Rf in the same solvent — used like a fingerprint.
Crystallisation
Separates a dissolved solid from its solution. Solution is heated to evaporate solvent and concentrate it → cooled slowly → crystals form as solubility decreases → crystals filtered off and dried. Produces purer and larger crystals than just evaporating to dryness.
Sublimation (separation)
Used when one component of a mixture can sublime directly from solid to gas. The sublimable substance vaporises on gentle heating and is collected on a cool surface above. The non-sublimable component stays at the bottom. Example: separating iodine from sand.
Desalination
Removing salt from seawater to make it drinkable. Two main methods: (1) distillation — boil seawater, collect steam; (2) reverse osmosis — high pressure forces water molecules through a semi-permeable membrane, leaving salt ions behind.

Scientific explanation — how chromatography actually works

Mechanism: There are two competing forces on each substance. The mobile phase (solvent moving up the paper) carries the substance upward. The stationary phase (the paper fibres) holds the substance back through intermolecular attractions. A substance that is very soluble in the solvent spends more time in the mobile phase and travels further up. A substance that is strongly attracted to the paper spends more time attached to the stationary phase and travels less far. Different substances in the mixture have different balances of these two forces — so they separate at different heights.

Criteria connections

Crit B: When designing a separation, state which technique you are using and why — link it directly to the property that allows the separation (particle size, boiling point, solubility, etc.).
Crit C: Calculate Rf values from a chromatogram. Identify unknown substances by comparing Rf to a reference. Identify how many substances are in a mixture by counting the number of spots.
Crit D: Evaluate which technique is most appropriate — e.g. "Simple distillation cannot separate two liquids with similar boiling points because they evaporate at almost the same temperature. Fractional distillation is needed because the column allows more separation steps."
Exam Questions — Unit 1▼
1 MARK State question
State what is meant by diffusion.
Model Answer

The net movement of particles from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration.

2 MARKS Explain question
Explain why a pure substance has a sharp melting point but an impure substance melts over a range of temperatures.
Model Answer

[1] In a pure substance, all particles are identical, so the same amount of energy is needed to break all the intermolecular bonds → melting occurs at one precise temperature.

[2] In an impure substance, the different-sized impurity particles disrupt the regular lattice structure, weakening it → less energy is needed to break the bonds → melting begins at a lower temperature and occurs over a range.

3 MARKS Explain question
Explain, in terms of particles, why a flat section (plateau) appears on a heating curve when a solid is being heated and begins to melt.
Model Answer

[1] During the flat section, heat energy is still being added to the substance.

[2] However, this energy is being used entirely to break the intermolecular bonds holding the solid lattice together, not to increase the kinetic energy of the particles.

[3] Because kinetic energy does not increase, the temperature does not rise — it stays constant until all bonds are broken and the substance is fully liquid.

5 MARKS Evaluate / discuss question
A student has a mixture of sand and salt dissolved in water. Describe and explain a method to obtain: (a) the sand, and (b) pure dry salt crystals from this mixture. [5]
Model Answer

[1 — method for sand] Filter the mixture through filter paper in a funnel. The sand (insoluble solid) is trapped as the residue on the filter paper.

[1 — why filtration works] Salt particles are dissolved at the molecular level and pass through the tiny pores in the filter paper; sand particles are too large to pass through.

[1 — concentrating] Heat the filtrate (salt solution) in an evaporating dish to evaporate most of the water, concentrating the salt solution.

[1 — crystallising] Remove from heat and allow to cool slowly — as the solution cools, the solubility of salt decreases and salt crystals form.

[1 — finishing] Filter the crystals, then dry them gently with filter paper or in a low-temperature oven. Do not evaporate to complete dryness, as this could decompose the salt or produce very small impure crystals.

Unit 2 · MYP 3

Periodic Table & Bonding

Atomic structure · Isotopes · Periodic trends · Bonding types · Carbon allotropes

Definitions — Atomic Structure & Atomic Models▼
Atom
The smallest particle of an element that can exist and still have the chemical properties of that element. Made up of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in shells.
Proton
Sub-atomic particle in the nucleus. Relative charge +1, relative mass 1. The number of protons defines which element an atom is.
Neutron
Sub-atomic particle in the nucleus. Relative charge 0 (neutral), relative mass 1. Contributes to mass but not charge.
Electron
Sub-atomic particle orbiting the nucleus in shells. Relative charge −1, relative mass ≈ 0 (1/1840 of a proton). Number of electrons = number of protons in a neutral atom.
Atomic number (Z)
The number of protons in an atom's nucleus. Unique to each element. Also equals the number of electrons in a neutral atom. Defines the element's position in the periodic table.
Mass number (A)
The total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus. Number of neutrons = A − Z.
Electron shell / energy level
A region around the nucleus where electrons are found. Shell 1 (innermost): max 2 electrons. Shell 2: max 8. Shell 3: max 8 (at MYP level). Electrons always fill from the lowest shell first.
Valence electrons
Electrons in the outermost shell of an atom. These are the electrons involved in bonding. The number of valence electrons = the group number (for Groups 1–7). They determine how an atom will bond.
Isotopes
Atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons — therefore different mass numbers. Same chemical properties (same electron arrangement), slightly different physical properties (different mass).
Average atomic mass (Ar)
The weighted average mass of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, relative to carbon-12. Calculated as: Ar = Σ(isotope mass × % abundance) ÷ 100.

Scientific explanation — why isotopes have same chemical but different physical properties

Same chemical properties: Chemical behaviour depends entirely on the number and arrangement of electrons. Isotopes of the same element have identical electron configurations, so they form the same bonds and undergo the same reactions — they are chemically indistinguishable.
Different physical properties: Physical properties like mass, density, and rate of diffusion depend on the mass of the particle. Since isotopes have different numbers of neutrons, they have different masses. A heavier isotope diffuses more slowly (Graham's Law: lighter particles move faster at the same temperature).

History of atomic models — why each was replaced

ModelScientistWhat it saidEvidence that replaced it

Criteria connections

Crit D: "Evaluate how the atomic model changed over time." — Always explain what evidence caused each change, not just what the new model said. Science progresses when new experimental evidence cannot be explained by the existing model.
Definitions — Periodic Table & Periodic Trends▼
Period
A horizontal row in the periodic table. The period number equals the number of electron shells. All elements in Period 3 have 3 electron shells.
Group
A vertical column in the periodic table. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons → similar chemical properties.
Transition metals
Elements in Groups 3–12, between the main groups. Properties: high melting points, hard, dense, form coloured compounds, can have multiple oxidation states, good catalysts. Examples: iron (Fe), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn).
Noble gases (Group 0)
Helium, neon, argon, etc. Have a full outer electron shell (2 for He, 8 for others) → extremely stable → almost no chemical reactivity. Exist as single atoms (monatomic). Used as inert atmospheres and in lighting.
Atomic radius
The size of an atom — half the distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms of the same element. Decreases across a period (more protons pull electrons closer); increases down a group (more shells).
Ionisation energy (1st)
The energy needed to remove one electron from a neutral atom in the gas phase. Increases across a period (more protons, smaller atom = stronger pull on electrons); decreases down a group (outer electron further from nucleus, more shielded).
Electronegativity
A measure of an atom's ability to attract a shared pair of electrons towards itself in a covalent bond. Increases across a period; decreases down a group. Fluorine has the highest electronegativity of all elements.
Shielding effect
Inner electron shells partially block the attractive force of the nucleus from the outer electrons. More inner shells = more shielding = outer electrons feel less nuclear attraction = easier to remove (lower ionisation energy).

Scientific explanation — periodic trends in detail

Why atomic radius decreases across a period: As you move left to right across a period, each successive element has one more proton in the nucleus. More protons = stronger positive charge = stronger attraction pulling all electrons closer to the nucleus. The number of shells stays the same across a period, so there is no extra shielding. The result is a smaller atom.
Why ionisation energy generally increases across a period: More protons → stronger nuclear pull on the outer electrons → more energy needed to pull one away. The outer electrons are also in the same shell (same distance, same shielding), so the increasing nuclear charge has a direct effect.
Why ionisation energy decreases down a group: Going down a group, each element has one more electron shell. The outer electron is further from the nucleus AND is shielded by more inner electron shells. Both factors reduce the attractive force on the outer electron → it takes less energy to remove it.
Definitions — Bonding Types▼
Ionic bond
Formed between a metal and a non-metal. The metal atom loses one or more electrons → becomes a positive ion (cation). The non-metal atom gains those electrons → becomes a negative ion (anion). The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond.
Covalent bond
Formed between two non-metals. Both atoms share a pair of electrons. The shared electrons are attracted to both nuclei simultaneously — this attraction holds the atoms together. Can be single (1 pair), double (2 pairs), or triple (3 pairs).
Metallic bond
Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a "sea" of delocalised electrons that move freely throughout the structure. The positively charged metal ions (cations) are attracted to this electron sea. This attraction holds the metal together.
Giant ionic lattice
The structure of ionic compounds — millions of alternating positive and negative ions arranged in a regular 3D structure. All ions are attracted to their neighbours. This is why ionic compounds have very high melting points — many strong bonds must be broken.
Simple molecular structure
Structure of many covalent compounds — consists of small, discrete molecules. The covalent bonds within each molecule are strong. But the forces between molecules (intermolecular forces) are weak. It is these weak forces that are overcome on melting/boiling — hence low melting points.
Giant covalent structure
A structure where millions of atoms are joined by a continuous network of strong covalent bonds throughout the entire structure. No small molecules. Every bond must be broken to melt it → extremely high melting points. Examples: diamond, graphite, SiO₂.

Scientific explanations — properties from structure

Why ionic compounds conduct electricity when molten or dissolved, but not when solid? In the solid state, ions are locked in fixed positions in the lattice — they cannot move to carry charge. When melted or dissolved in water, the ionic lattice breaks down and ions become free to move. Moving charged particles = electric current.
Why metals are malleable (can be hammered into shapes)? When a force is applied, layers of metal ions slide over each other. The delocalised electron sea moves with them and maintains the bonding throughout — the structure does not break. This is unlike ionic compounds where shifting layers brings like charges together, causing repulsion and shattering.
Why simple molecular substances have low melting points? To melt the substance, you only need to overcome the weak intermolecular forces between the molecules — the strong covalent bonds within the molecules are NOT broken. Weak forces require little energy → low melting point.

Diamond cannot conduct. Graphene is too expensive at scale.
Exam Questions — Unit 2▼
1 MARK
State what is meant by an isotope.
Model Answer

Atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (and therefore different mass numbers).

2 MARKS
Explain why graphite can conduct electricity but diamond cannot, even though both are made only from carbon atoms.
Model Answer

[1] In graphite, each carbon atom forms only 3 covalent bonds, leaving one electron per atom delocalised (free to move) between the layers — these mobile electrons carry electric charge.

[2] In diamond, each carbon forms 4 covalent bonds — all valence electrons are used in bonding, so there are no free electrons available to carry charge.

3 MARKS
Explain why the first ionisation energy of sodium (Na) is lower than the first ionisation energy of chlorine (Cl). Both are in Period 3.
Model Answer

[1] Chlorine has more protons (17) than sodium (11), so the nucleus of chlorine exerts a stronger attractive force on the outer electrons.

[2] Both elements have electrons in the same shell (shell 3), so shielding from inner shells is the same for both.

[3] Because chlorine's outer electron is attracted more strongly to the nucleus, more energy is needed to remove it → chlorine has a higher first ionisation energy than sodium.

5 MARKS
Explain why ionic compounds have high melting points and conduct electricity when molten, but simple molecular covalent substances have low melting points and do not conduct electricity in any state. [5]
Model Answer

[1 — ionic MP] Ionic compounds form giant lattice structures with strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions in all directions — a large amount of energy is needed to overcome these forces, giving a high melting point.

[2 — ionic conduction] When molten, the ionic lattice breaks down and ions become free to move and carry charge — so molten ionic compounds conduct electricity.

[3 — why not solid ionic] In the solid state, ions are fixed in position and cannot move → solid ionic compounds do not conduct.

[4 — molecular MP] Simple molecular substances have weak intermolecular forces between their molecules (not the strong covalent bonds within). Only the weak intermolecular forces need to be overcome to melt the substance → low melting point.

[5 — molecular no conduction] Molecules have no overall charge and carry no free electrons or mobile ions in any state (solid, liquid, or gas) → simple molecular substances cannot conduct electricity.

Unit 3

Reaction Kinetics

Rate of reaction · Collision theory · Equilibrium · Graph skills · Reactivity series · Corrosion

Definitions — Rate of Reaction & Collision Theory▼
Rate of reaction
A measure of how quickly reactants are converted into products. Rate = quantity of reactant used (or product formed) ÷ time. Units: g/s, cm³/s, mol/s.
Activation energy (Eₐ)
The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur. It is the energy needed to break the existing bonds in the reactants so new bonds can form.
Collision theory
Reactions occur when particles collide with: (1) the correct orientation (right parts facing each other), and (2) energy ≥ activation energy. Collisions that meet both conditions are called effective collisions. Rate of reaction is proportional to the frequency of effective collisions.
Catalyst
A substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being permanently changed or consumed. It provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy — so more particles have enough energy to react. It does not change the products or the energy change of the reaction.
Concentration
The amount of solute dissolved per unit volume of solution. Units: mol/dm³. Higher concentration = more particles in the same space → more frequent collisions → faster rate.
Surface area
The total area of a solid that is exposed and available for reaction. Increasing surface area (e.g. grinding a solid into powder) exposes more particles at the surface → more collisions possible per second → faster rate.

Scientific explanations — every factor with collision theory reasoning

Temperature increase → faster rate because: Higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy → they move faster → they collide more frequently → AND a much greater proportion of particles now have energy ≥ the activation energy. Both effects increase the number of effective collisions per second. A 10°C rise approximately doubles the reaction rate.
Concentration increase → faster rate because: More solute particles are present per unit volume → particles are closer together → they collide more frequently → more effective collisions per second. (The energy of individual collisions does not change — just the frequency.)
Surface area increase → faster rate because: When a solid is broken into smaller pieces, the same total mass has more particles exposed at the surface. Only surface particles can collide with the other reactant. More surface particles available → more collisions per second → faster rate.
Catalyst → faster rate because: The catalyst provides a different reaction pathway — one with a lower activation energy. This means a larger proportion of the particles already have enough energy to react (you don't need to raise temperature). The catalyst is not consumed — it can continue to work. Examples: MnO₂ for H₂O₂ decomposition; iron for the Haber process.
What a catalyst does NOT do: It does not make the reaction more exothermic or endothermic. It does not change the equilibrium position. It does not increase the energy of the particles. It only lowers the activation energy — providing a shortcut.
Definitions — Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle▼
Reversible reaction
A reaction that can proceed in both the forward direction (reactants → products) and the reverse direction (products → reactants). Shown by the symbol ⇌.
Dynamic equilibrium
The state reached in a closed system when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. Concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal). Both reactions are still occurring — the equilibrium is "dynamic" (not static).
Le Chatelier's principle
If a change is applied to a system at equilibrium, the equilibrium will shift in the direction that opposes (counteracts) that change. Used to predict how concentration, temperature, and pressure affect equilibrium position.

Le Chatelier's predictions — with full scientific explanation

Definitions — Graph & Data Skills▼
Independent variable (IV)
The variable you deliberately change in an experiment. Always plotted on the x-axis. Only one IV per fair test.
Dependent variable (DV)
The variable you measure in response to the IV. Always plotted on the y-axis. Changes as a result of the IV.
Controlled variable (CV)
All variables that are kept constant to ensure a fair test. If CVs change, you cannot be sure it was the IV causing the change in DV.
Line of best fit
A single smooth line or curve drawn through the middle of data points on a scatter graph. It does not connect dot-to-dot. It shows the overall trend. Minimises total distance of all points from the line.
Outlier / anomalous result
A data point that does not follow the general trend — it lies far from the line of best fit. Circle it on the graph. Do not include it in the line of best fit. Suggest a reason (e.g. measurement error, contaminated sample).
Gradient
The slope of a line on a graph. Gradient = Δy ÷ Δx. Use a large triangle (at least half the line). Always include units. On a rate graph, gradient = rate of reaction at that moment.
Reliability
How consistent / repeatable results are. Reliable data shows little variation between repeats. Shown by points clustered tightly around the line of best fit.
Validity
Whether the experiment actually measures what it claims to measure. An investigation is valid if the method correctly controls all variables except the IV.

Criteria connections

Crit B: State your IV, DV, and at least 3 CVs. Explain how you will control each CV. Explain why the method is safe. Predict the outcome based on scientific reasoning.
Crit C: Plot data correctly (IV on x-axis, DV on y-axis, correct scale using most of the grid, labelled axes with units). Draw line of best fit. Calculate gradient with correct units. Identify and circle outliers. Comment on the trend.
Crit D: Evaluate reliability (how close are repeats?), validity (were all CVs controlled?), and suggest specific improvements (e.g. "Use a light sensor instead of visual judgement to determine the endpoint more objectively and reduce human error").
Definitions — Reactivity Series, Extraction & Corrosion▼
Reactivity series
A list of metals (and hydrogen) arranged in order of decreasing reactivity. Most reactive at the top: K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Pb, H, Cu, Ag, Au. More reactive metals lose electrons more easily and react more vigorously with water, acid, and oxygen.
Displacement reaction
A reaction where a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution. The more reactive metal loses electrons (oxidised); the less reactive metal ion gains electrons (reduced). This is a redox reaction.
Ore
A naturally occurring rock from which a metal can be extracted economically. Most metals exist as oxides, sulfides, or carbonates in ores — they must be reduced to obtain the pure metal.
Reduction (extraction)
The process of removing oxygen from a metal oxide (or gaining electrons) to obtain the pure metal. Carbon or carbon monoxide acts as the reducing agent in the blast furnace.
Electrolysis (extraction)
Using electrical energy to decompose an ionic compound to extract very reactive metals (Al, Na, Mg) that cannot be reduced by carbon. The compound must be molten or dissolved to free the ions.
Corrosion
The gradual destruction of a metal by chemical reaction with its environment. Most commonly oxidation by oxygen and water. Rusting is the specific corrosion of iron.
Rusting
The corrosion of iron. Requires both water AND oxygen simultaneously — neither alone causes rust. Product: hydrated iron(III) oxide (Fe₂O₃·xH₂O). Salt water accelerates rusting (dissolved ions improve electrical conductivity of the water layer).
Sacrificial protection
Attaching a more reactive metal (zinc or magnesium) to iron. The more reactive metal corrodes preferentially — it is "sacrificed" to protect the iron. The iron cannot corrode as long as the sacrificial metal remains. Used on underground pipes and ships' hulls.
Galvanising
Coating iron with a layer of zinc. Provides two forms of protection: (1) physical barrier (zinc coat keeps water/O₂ away), and (2) sacrificial protection (even if coating is scratched, zinc corrodes before iron).

Scientific explanation — why galvanising is better than tin plating

Galvanising (zinc coat): Zinc is MORE reactive than iron. So even if the zinc coating is scratched and both metals are exposed to water and oxygen, zinc corrodes first (it is the sacrificial anode). The iron underneath is protected even when the surface is damaged.
Tin plating (tin can): Tin is LESS reactive than iron. If the tin coating is scratched and iron is exposed, iron is now the more reactive metal and corrodes first — rapidly. Tin plating only works as long as the physical barrier is intact.

Criteria connections

Crit D: "Which method of corrosion prevention is most suitable for a ship's hull in seawater?" → Sacrificial anodes (zinc/magnesium bolted to hull) + paint coating. 5 MARKS
The Haber process produces ammonia: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g). The forward reaction is exothermic. Explain, using Le Chatelier's principle, the effect of (a) increasing pressure and (b) increasing temperature on the yield of ammonia. Then explain why a compromise temperature of 450°C is used in industry despite not giving the highest yield. [5]
Model Answer

[1 — pressure effect on yield] Increasing pressure shifts the equilibrium towards the side with fewer moles of gas. The left side has 1 + 3 = 4 moles of gas; the right has 2 moles. So increasing pressure shifts the equilibrium to the right → yield of ammonia increases.

[2 — temperature effect on yield] The forward reaction is exothermic. Increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium towards the endothermic (reverse) direction, to absorb the extra heat. This shifts equilibrium to the left → yield of ammonia decreases.

[3 — so why not use low temperature?] A lower temperature would give a higher yield (equilibrium favours forward reaction), but the reaction rate would be extremely slow — it would take too long to produce ammonia at a commercially useful rate.

[4 — compromise] 450°C is a compromise — the rate is fast enough to be economically viable, even though the yield at equilibrium is not the theoretical maximum.

[5 — catalyst role] An iron catalyst is used to increase the rate of reaction at this temperature without affecting the equilibrium position — helping reach equilibrium faster and making the process more efficient overall.

Unit 4

Stoichiometry

Balancing equations · Conservation of mass · The mole · Avogadro · Molar calculations · Gas laws

Definitions — Equations & Conservation of Mass▼
Conservation of mass
In any chemical reaction, atoms are neither created nor destroyed — only rearranged. Therefore the total mass of reactants always equals the total mass of products. This is why equations must be balanced.
Balanced equation
A chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides. Only coefficients (numbers in front) can be changed to balance — never subscripts (numbers within the formula).
Empirical formula
The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. Found by dividing % composition by relative atomic mass, then simplifying. Example: glucose empirical formula = CH₂O.
Molecular formula
The actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule. May be a multiple of the empirical formula. Example: glucose molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆ (6 × CH₂O).

Why mass appears to change — and why it doesn't really

Burning magnesium (mass increases): Mass appears to increase because magnesium reacts with oxygen from the air. The oxygen atoms from the air are not being weighed initially — but they are now part of the magnesium oxide product. Total mass (Mg + O₂) = mass of MgO. Mass is conserved.
Thermal decomposition (mass decreases): If a gas is produced and escapes the container, the measured mass decreases. Example: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. The CO₂ gas escapes. The mass of the solid in the container decreases. But if you count the escaped gas, total mass is still conserved.
Definitions — The Mole & All Molar Calculations▼
Mole (mol)
The SI unit for amount of substance. 1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or formula units). The mole bridges the gap between the atomic scale and the macroscopic scale.
Avogadro's constant (Nₐ)
The number of particles in exactly one mole: 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹. Named after Amedeo Avogadro. This number was chosen so that 1 mole of any substance has a mass in grams equal to its relative formula mass.
Molar mass (Mr)
The mass of one mole of a substance. Numerically equal to the relative formula mass (Mr), but in units of g/mol. Example: Mr of H₂O = 2(1) + 16 = 18 g/mol. So 1 mole of water has mass 18 g.
Molar concentration
The number of moles of solute dissolved per 1 dm³ (1 litre) of solution. Units: mol/dm³ (also written mol/L or M). Formula: c = n ÷ V (V must be in dm³).
Molar volume of a gas
At room temperature and pressure (RTP: 25°C, 1 atm), 1 mole of any gas occupies 24 dm³ (24,000 cm³). This is the same for all gases regardless of their mass.

All four mole equations

n = m ÷ Mr → moles from mass (m in grams, Mr in g/mol) n = c × V → moles from concentration (c in mol/dm³, V in dm³!) n = V(gas) ÷ 24 → moles from gas volume at RTP (V in dm³) N = n × Nₐ → number of particles from moles KEY CONVERSIONS: cm³ → dm³: divide by 1000 dm³ → cm³: multiply by 1000 K = °C + 273

How to do a stoichiometry calculation — step by step

1
Write the balanced equation. Write the mole ratio underneath the relevant substances.
2
Calculate moles of the substance you are given information about (use n = m/Mr or n = cV).
3
Scale using the mole ratio from the equation to find moles of the substance you want.
4
Convert moles back to mass, volume, or concentration as the question requires.
Worked example: What mass of water is produced when 4 g of hydrogen burns in excess oxygen?
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (ratio: 2 mol H₂ : 2 mol H₂O = 1:1)
n(H₂) = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 mol
n(H₂O) = 2 mol (1:1 ratio)
m(H₂O) = 2 × 18 = 36 g
Definitions — Gas Laws▼
Boyle's Law
At constant temperature, the pressure and volume of a gas are inversely proportional: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂. Explanation: if volume decreases, particles are in a smaller space → they hit the walls more often → pressure increases.
Charles's Law
At constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (in Kelvin): V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂. Explanation: higher temperature gives particles more energy → they move faster and push the walls harder → volume expands to maintain constant pressure.
Gay-Lussac's / Pressure Law
At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂. Explanation: higher temperature → faster particles → hit walls more often and with more force → higher pressure.
Combined Gas Law
When all three variables change: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂. Used for closed systems. Temperature MUST be in Kelvin.
Absolute zero
0 K (−273°C) — the theoretical temperature at which particles have zero kinetic energy and the volume of a gas would be zero. Cannot be reached in practice.

Real-life applications — linked to the law

ApplicationWhich lawExplanation
Bicycle pump / syringeBoyle's LawPushing the plunger decreases volume → pressure increases → gas is pushed out.
Scuba diving bendsBoyle's LawAt depth, high pressure compresses gas in lungs. Ascending too fast → pressure drops → gas expands rapidly in bloodstream → bubbles form in tissues (decompression sickness).
Hot air balloonCharles's LawHeating air inside the balloon increases its volume → makes it less dense than surrounding cool air → balloon rises.
Aerosol cans in hot carPressure LawTemperature rises → gas pressure inside the can increases → risk of explosion.

Carbon — The Element of Life

Organic chemistry · IUPAC naming · Homologous series · Energy changes · Combustion

Definitions — Organic Chemistry & Homologous Series▼
Organic chemistry
The chemistry of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon is unique because it can form 4 covalent bonds and bond to other carbon atoms in long chains, rings, and branches (called catenation) → millions of different organic compounds exist.
Homologous series
A family of organic compounds that: share the same functional group; follow the same general formula; differ by –CH₂– between successive members; show gradual trends in physical properties; and have similar chemical properties.
Functional group
The specific group of atoms in an organic molecule responsible for its characteristic chemical reactions. The functional group determines which homologous series a compound belongs to.
Saturated compound
A compound with only single covalent bonds between carbon atoms. Cannot undergo addition reactions. Example: all alkanes.
Unsaturated compound
A compound containing at least one C=C double bond (or triple bond). Can undergo addition reactions. Example: all alkenes. Test: decolourises orange bromine water → colourless.

Why boiling point increases with chain length

Explanation: Longer carbon chains have more electrons → stronger London dispersion forces (temporary dipole-induced dipole forces) between molecules → more energy needed to separate the molecules → higher boiling point. This is why methane (CH₄) is a gas at room temperature but hexadecane (C₁₆H₃₄, found in diesel) is a liquid.

Ester formation — the science behind it

Esterification
A condensation reaction between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid to form an ester and water. Requires an acid catalyst (e.g. H₂SO₄) and gentle heating. The reaction is reversible. Example: ethanol + ethanoic acid ⇌ ethyl ethanoate + water. Esters have characteristic fruity smells.
Naming esters: First word comes from the alcohol (change -ol → -yl). Second word comes from the acid (change -anoic acid → -anoate). Example: propanol + ethanoic acid → propyl ethanoate + water.
Definitions — Energy Changes in Reactions▼
Exothermic reaction
A reaction that releases energy to the surroundings. Temperature of the surroundings increases. ΔH is negative (−). Products have lower energy than reactants. Energy released when new bonds form > energy needed to break old bonds.
Endothermic reaction
A reaction that absorbs energy from the surroundings. Temperature of the surroundings decreases. ΔH is positive (+). Products have higher energy than reactants. Energy needed to break bonds > energy released forming new bonds.
Bond energy
The energy required to break one mole of a particular covalent bond in the gas phase. Breaking bonds always requires energy (endothermic). Forming bonds always releases energy (exothermic).
ΔH (enthalpy change)
The heat energy change for a reaction at constant pressure. ΔH = Σ(bond energies broken) − Σ(bond energies formed). Negative = exothermic; positive = endothermic.
Activation energy (Eₐ)
On an energy profile diagram, it is the energy difference between the reactants and the peak (transition state). A catalyst lowers this peak, providing an alternative pathway with lower Eₐ.

The critical distinction — bonds breaking vs intermolecular forces

Common mistake to avoid: When a simple molecular substance melts or boils, the covalent bonds inside the molecules are NOT broken. Only the weak intermolecular forces between molecules are overcome. This is why simple molecular substances have low melting points. Covalent bonds are only broken during chemical reactions (e.g. combustion).

Scientific explanation — why CO is dangerous

Carbon monoxide (CO) toxicity mechanism: CO binds to haemoglobin in red blood cells with an affinity approximately 200 times greater than oxygen. This forms carboxyhaemoglobin — a stable compound that cannot carry oxygen. The cells are deprived of oxygen → tissue damage → death at high concentrations. CO is colourless and odourless — there is no sensory warning. This is why CO detectors are essential in homes with gas appliances.

The combustion of methane: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Using bond energies (C–H = 412, O=O = 496, C=O = 743, O–H = 463 kJ/mol), calculate the enthalpy change ΔH and state whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic.
Model Answer

[1 — bonds broken] Break: 4 × C–H = 4 × 412 = 1648 kJ. Break: 2 × O=O = 2 × 496 = 992 kJ. Total energy input = 1648 + 992 = 2640 kJ.

[2 — bonds formed] Form: 2 × C=O (in CO₂) = 2 × 743 = 1486 kJ. Form: 4 × O–H (in 2H₂O) = 4 × 463 = 1852 kJ. Total energy released = 1486 + 1852 = 3338 kJ.

5 MARKS

[2 — counter-argument / limitation of biofuel] However, the production of bioethanol still requires energy (for farming machinery, fertiliser production, distillation) — often from fossil fuels. This makes it not truly carbon-neutral in practice.

[3 — land use / food security] Growing energy crops (e.g. sugar cane, maize) requires large areas of agricultural land. This competes with food production → could raise food prices and threaten food security, particularly in developing countries.

[4 — energy density / practicality] Ethanol has a lower energy density than petrol (releases less energy per litre burned) → vehicles need larger fuel tanks or more frequent refuelling to travel the same distance, reducing efficiency.

[5 — overall evaluation] Biofuels offer clear advantages in reducing fossil fuel dependence and in theory lowering net CO₂ emissions, but they are not a perfect solution. A combination of energy sources (including renewables for electricity) alongside improved fuel efficiency is likely more effective than biofuels alone.

Unit 6 · MYP 2

From Acids to Electrons

Acids & bases · pH · Salts · Redox · Electrolysis · Atmosphere · Climate

Definitions — Acids, Bases & pH▼
Acid
A substance that donates protons (H⁺ ions) when dissolved in water (Brønsted-Lowry definition). Produces H⁺(aq) ions in solution. Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CH₃COOH.
Base
A substance that accepts protons (H⁺ ions). Metal oxides and metal hydroxides are bases. They react with acids to form a salt and water (neutralisation).
Alkali
A soluble base. When dissolved in water, an alkali produces OH⁻ ions. Examples: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₃(aq).
Neutralisation
The reaction between an acid and a base to form a salt and water. The H⁺ ions from the acid combine with the OH⁻ ions from the base: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l). This ionic equation is the same for all acid-base neutralisations.
pH scale
A measure of the concentration of H⁺ ions in solution. pH 0–6 = acidic; pH 7 = neutral; pH 8–14 = alkaline. The scale is logarithmic — each unit change represents a 10× change in H⁺ concentration. pH 3 is 10 times more acidic than pH 4, and 100 times more acidic than pH 5.
Strong acid
An acid that completely ionises in water — all molecules donate their proton. HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻ (one-way arrow — complete). Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃.
Weak acid
An acid that only partially ionises in water — most molecules remain un-ionised. CH₃COOH ⇌ H⁺ + CH₃COO⁻ (equilibrium — incomplete). Lower H⁺ concentration → higher pH than a strong acid of the same concentration.

Why logarithmic scale matters in the exam

pH is logarithmic — this is a common exam trap: If pH decreases by 1, the H⁺ concentration increases by 10×. If pH decreases by 2, the H⁺ concentration increases by 100×. So pH 1 has 10× more H⁺ than pH 2, and 1000× more H⁺ than pH 4. This is why even small pH changes in the ocean (ocean acidification) represent large changes in actual acidity.
Definitions — Reactions of Acids & Salts▼
Salt
An ionic compound formed when the H⁺ from an acid is replaced by a metal ion (or ammonium ion NH₄⁺). The name and formula of the salt depends on the acid used and the base/metal used.
Titration
A technique used to determine the exact concentration of an acid or alkali by adding a measured volume of one solution from a burette to a known volume of the other (in a conical flask with indicator) until the reaction is exactly complete (the end point).
End point
The point in a titration when the indicator changes colour permanently — indicating that the acid and alkali have exactly reacted in stoichiometric amounts. The number of moles of acid = the number of moles of alkali (adjusted for mole ratio).
Precipitation reaction
A reaction where two soluble ionic solutions are mixed and an insoluble salt (precipitate) forms. The precipitate forms because the product is insoluble according to solubility rules.

Definitions — Redox Reactions▼
Oxidation
Loss of electrons (OIL — Oxidation Is Loss). Alternatively: gain of oxygen, or loss of hydrogen. Oxidation state increases when a substance is oxidised.
Reduction
Gain of electrons (RIG — Reduction Is Gain). Alternatively: loss of oxygen, or gain of hydrogen. Oxidation state decreases when a substance is reduced.
Redox reaction
A reaction in which both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously. One substance loses electrons (is oxidised) and another gains those same electrons (is reduced). Electrons are transferred between species.
Oxidising agent
The substance that causes another substance to be oxidised by accepting electrons from it. The oxidising agent itself is reduced in the process.
Reducing agent
The substance that causes another substance to be reduced by donating electrons to it. The reducing agent itself is oxidised in the process.
Oxidation state
A number assigned to an atom representing its degree of oxidation. Key rules: uncombined element = 0; oxygen in compounds = −2; hydrogen in compounds = +1; ions = charge; sum in a neutral compound = 0.

The key confusion — oxidising agent gets REDUCED

Always confuses students: The oxidising agent CAUSES oxidation in something else → but it itself GAINS electrons → so it itself is REDUCED. The reducing agent CAUSES reduction in something else → but it itself LOSES electrons → so it itself is OXIDISED. Remember: "the agent does the opposite of its name."
Example: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu Zn: oxidation state 0 → +2 (INCREASES) → Zn is OXIDISED → Zn is the REDUCING AGENT Cu²⁺: oxidation state +2 → 0 (DECREASES) → Cu²⁺ is REDUCED → Cu²⁺/CuSO₄ is the OXIDISING AGENT Half equations: Oxidation: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ Reduction: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Definitions — Electrolysis▼
Electrolysis
The decomposition of an ionic compound using electrical energy. Requires the compound to be molten or dissolved in water so that ions are free to move. Used to extract reactive metals and to produce important chemicals.
Electrolyte
The ionic compound being electrolysed. It conducts electricity in the molten or aqueous state by the movement of ions.
Cathode
The negative electrode in electrolysis. Cations (positive ions) are attracted to it and gain electrons here. Reduction occurs at the cathode: cation + e⁻ → product.
Anode
The positive electrode in electrolysis. Anions (negative ions) are attracted to it and lose electrons here. Oxidation occurs at the anode: anion → product + e⁻.
Discharge of ions
When an ion arrives at an electrode and gains or loses electrons to become a neutral atom or molecule. If multiple ions are present, the one that is most easily oxidised/reduced is preferentially discharged.
Electroplating
Using electrolysis to coat an object with a thin layer of metal. The object is the cathode (metal ions are deposited onto it). The plating metal is the anode (it dissolves to replenish the solution). The electrolyte is a solution of the plating metal's salt.

Definitions — Atmosphere, Greenhouse Effect & Ozone▼
Greenhouse effect
The natural warming of Earth's surface by certain gases in the atmosphere that absorb outgoing infrared (heat) radiation and re-emit it back toward Earth. Without it, Earth's average temperature would be −18°C instead of +15°C.
Enhanced greenhouse effect
The additional warming caused by human activities increasing the concentrations of greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) beyond natural levels → trapping more infrared radiation → global warming.
Greenhouse gas
A gas that absorbs infrared radiation. Main ones: CO₂ (from fossil fuel combustion, deforestation), CH₄ (from livestock, landfill, rice paddies), N₂O (from fertilisers), water vapour (natural), CFCs (industrial — now banned).
Global warming
The increase in Earth's average surface temperature over recent decades, attributed mainly to the enhanced greenhouse effect. Consequences: rising sea levels, more extreme weather, ecosystem disruption, ice cap melting, ocean acidification.
Ozone layer
A layer of ozone (O₃) in the stratosphere (~15–35 km altitude) that absorbs most of the harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation from the sun. Without it, increased UV radiation would cause skin cancer, cataracts, and damage to ecosystems.
Ozone depletion
The thinning of the ozone layer caused by CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons). In the stratosphere, UV breaks CFCs apart, releasing Cl• free radicals that catalytically destroy ozone: Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂, then ClO• + O• → Cl• + O₂. One Cl• atom can destroy thousands of O₃ molecules.
Ocean acidification
The decrease in ocean pH caused by CO₂ dissolving in seawater to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which then partially ionises, increasing H⁺ concentration. CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. This dissolves coral and shell material (CaCO₃) and disrupts marine food chains.
Acid rain
Rain with a lower pH than normal (~5.6), caused by SO₂ and NOₓ dissolving in rainwater to form sulfurous/sulfuric acid and nitric acid. SO₂ comes from burning fossil fuels containing sulfur. NOₓ comes from car engines and power stations (N₂ reacts with O₂ at high temperatures).

Scientific mechanism — greenhouse effect step by step

Full mechanism: (1) Short-wave visible light from the sun passes through the atmosphere (greenhouse gases do not absorb it). (2) Earth's surface absorbs this radiation and warms up. (3) Earth re-emits this energy as long-wave infrared (heat) radiation. (4) Greenhouse gas molecules absorb this outgoing infrared radiation — their bonds vibrate and energy is absorbed. (5) The molecules then re-emit infrared in all directions — including back toward Earth. (6) This traps heat in the lower atmosphere, warming the surface beyond what solar input alone would cause. (7) More greenhouse gases = more infrared absorbed and re-emitted back = more warming.

Scientific mechanism — why CFCs are uniquely destructive to ozone

Why it's a chain reaction (catalytic): Chlorine free radicals (Cl•) are not consumed — they are regenerated at the end of each cycle. One Cl• atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules before it is finally removed from the system. This is why even small amounts of CFCs released into the atmosphere cause disproportionately large ozone depletion.

Exam Questions — Unit 6▼
1 MARK
State what is meant by an oxidising agent.
Model Answer

A substance that accepts electrons from another substance (causing the other substance to be oxidised), while being reduced itself.

2 MARKS
Explain why the electrolysis of molten sodium chloride produces different products at each electrode.
Model Answer

[1 — cathode] At the cathode (negative electrode), Na⁺ ions are attracted and gain electrons: Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na. Reduction occurs, producing sodium metal.

[2 — anode] At the anode (positive electrode), Cl⁻ ions are attracted and lose electrons: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻. Oxidation occurs, producing chlorine gas.

3 MARKS
Explain the mechanism by which increased CO₂ emissions are causing ocean acidification, and state one consequence for marine organisms.
Model Answer

[1 — mechanism] CO₂ from burning fossil fuels dissolves in seawater to form carbonic acid: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃. Carbonic acid partially ionises, releasing H⁺ ions: H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. This increases the concentration of H⁺ ions → the pH of the ocean decreases (becomes more acidic).

[2 — logarithmic scale importance] Because pH is a logarithmic scale, even a small numerical decrease represents a large increase in actual acidity — the current drop of ~0.1 pH units since industrialisation represents approximately a 26% increase in H⁺ concentration.

[3 — consequence] Organisms such as corals and shellfish build shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). In more acidic conditions, CaCO₃ dissolves: CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻. This prevents shell formation and causes existing shells/coral to dissolve → coral reef decline and collapse of marine food chains.

5 MARKS
Discuss and evaluate the scientific evidence for the enhanced greenhouse effect and the consequences for the environment and society. In your answer, suggest one way individuals and one way governments could reduce the impact. [5]
Model Answer

[1 — mechanism] The enhanced greenhouse effect occurs because human activities — primarily burning fossil fuels and deforestation — have increased atmospheric CO₂ from ~280 ppm pre-industrialisation to over 420 ppm today. CO₂ and other greenhouse gases (CH₄, N₂O) absorb outgoing infrared radiation from Earth's surface and re-emit it back, trapping more heat than natural levels would and raising global average temperatures.

[2 — evidence] Scientific evidence includes: rising global average temperatures (NASA records show ~1.1°C rise since 1880), melting polar ice caps and glaciers, rising sea levels, and shifts in species ranges. The IPCC reports that it is "unequivocal" that human influence has warmed the climate.

[3 — environmental consequences] Consequences include: rising sea levels threatening coastal cities and island nations, more intense hurricanes and droughts, coral reef bleaching from warming and acidification, and disruption of ecosystems as species cannot adapt fast enough to the pace of change.

[4 — individual action] Individuals can reduce their carbon footprint by using public transport or electric vehicles instead of petrol cars, reducing meat consumption (livestock produce significant CH₄), and lowering home energy use (switching to renewables or improving insulation).

[5 — government action + evaluation] Governments can impose carbon taxes (making fossil fuel use economically costly, incentivising alternatives), fund renewable energy infrastructure, and set mandatory emissions targets (as in the Paris Agreement, 2015 — aiming to limit warming to 1.5–2°C). However, effectiveness depends on international cooperation and enforcement — countries with developing economies argue they should not be limited in the same way as industrialised nations that caused most historical emissions, creating political barriers to action.
